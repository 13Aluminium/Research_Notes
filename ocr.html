Making of a Sanskrit OCR

Introduction

Sanskrit, one of the world’s oldest documented languages, is an Old Indo-Aryan language in which the most ancient documents are the Vedas, composed in what is called Vedic Sanskrit. Considered a holy language, Sanskrit was thought of as the “Language of the Gods” and is the original language of Hindu Scriptures.

The linguistic sophistication of Sanskrit is exemplified by Panini’s grammatical work, the Asthadhyayi, written around 1500 B.C. which is still unexcelled, representing one of the earliest and most comprehensive linguistic analyses in human history. Despite this rich heritage, the vast corpus of Sanskrit literature remains largely inaccessible in the digital formats.

Motivation

The digitization of Sanskrit texts represents a critical bottleneck in preserving and accessing India’a intellectual heritage. Currently, the majority of Sanskrit literature exists in physical mansuscript and printed texts, with limited digital avalability. While some scanning initiatives have been undertaken, these efforts typically produce image-based documents that present several accessibility challenges:

  1. Reading Difficulty: Scanned copies are often of poor quality, making textual
    analysis challenging
  1. Limited Searchability: Image-based documents cannot be indexed or searched
    effectively
  1. Accessibility Barriers: Visual impairments and varied device compatibility
    issues
  1. Scholarly Limitations: Inability to perform computational text analysis, cross-
    referencing, or automated translation

The development of robust Sanskrit OCR system would significantly enhance accessibility to these texts while enabling advanced computational analysis of Sanskrit literature.The proposed model should demonstrate capability in:
• Accurate text extraction from scanned manuscripts and printed materials
• Structural understanding of Sanskrit textual organization (particularly shloka
demarcation)
• Language differentiation between Sanskrit and closely related languages (Hindi,
Devanagari-based texts)

Technical Challenges and Research Roadblocks

Dataset Quality and Composition

The primary challenge lies in constructing a comprehensive training dataset. Unlike modern languages with abundant digital text, Sanskrit OCR requires specialized dataset addressing multiple variables:

Proposed Dataset Categories (minimum 5-6 distinct types):
• Historical manuscript variations
• Different printing styles and fonts
• Various paper qualities and aging conditions
• Multiple script styles (Devanagari variations)
• Mixed-language documents
• Different text layouts and formatting conventions

Language Disambiguation

Sanskrit and Hindi share the Devanagri Scirpt, creating classification challenges.
The model must distinguish between:
• Pure Sanskrit verses
• Hindi translations or commentary
• Mixed-language sections
• Sanskrit words in Hindi contexts

Shloka Boundary Detection

Accurate identification of verse boundaries is crucial for meaningful text
segmentation. While traditional markers (||2||, etc.) provide some guidance, the
model must learn to recognize:
• Explicit numerical markers
• Implicit poetic structure indicators
• Contextual verse transitions
• Incomplete or damaged boundary markers

Annotation Strategy and Data Preparation

The annotation methodology represents a critical design decision requiring careful
consideration of granularity levels:

  1. Word-level annotation: Highest precision but labor-intensive

  1. Morpheme-level annotation: Linguistically sophisticated but complex
  1. Half-shloka annotation: Balanced approach considering poetic structure
  1. Complete shloka annotation: Efficient but potentially less precise
    The selection of annotation strategy will significantly impact model performance and
    training efficiency.

Proposed Development Workflow

Stage A: Binary Classification Model

Develop a preliminary classifier to distinguish between shloka and non-shloka text
regions, enabling initial content filtering and reducing noise in subsequent
processing stages

Stage B: Image Preprocessing and Segmentation

Implement automated cropping algorithms to extract pure shloka images from
source documents, creating a refined dataset for specialized OCR training

Stage C: Parallel Corpus Construction

Generate annotated image-text pairs from segmented shloka images, establishing
the foundation for supervised learning approaches.

Stage D: Progressive Training Architecture

Implement a two-stage training methodology:

  1. Initial model training on Dataset A
  1. Validation and testing on Dataset B
  1. Incremental learning using Dataset A + high-confidence predictions from
    Dataset B
  1. Catastrophic forgetting prevention through progressive training techniques

Stage E: Hierarchical Recognition System

The final model architecture should demonstrate multi-level recognition capabilities:
E1: Primary Classification
• Shloka vs. non-shloka content identification
E2: Inter-shloka Segmentation
• Boundary detection between consecutive shlokas
E3: Intra-shloka Analysis
• Recognition of constituent parts within individual shlokas
• Syllable-level accuracy
