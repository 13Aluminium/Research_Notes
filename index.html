Sanskrit

Sanskrit’s Legacy

Sanskrit, one of the world’s oldest documented languages, is an Old Indo-Aryan language in which the most ancient documents are the Vedas, composed in what is called Vedic Sanskrit. Considered a holy language, Sanskrit was thought of as the “Language of the Gods” and is the original language of Hindu Scriptures.

The linguistic sophistication of Sanskrit is exemplified by Panini’s grammatical work, the Asthadhyayi, written around 1500 B.C. which is still unexcelled, representing one of the earliest and most comprehensive linguistic analyses in human history. Despite this rich heritage, the vast corpus of Sanskrit literature remains largely inaccessible in the digital formats.

Why Sanskrit is still Underdeveloped Digitally

Over the past decades, there have been important efforts to digitize and preserve Sanskrit:

  • GRETIL (Göttingen Register of Electronic Texts in Indian Languages): A well-known digital repository of Sanskrit e-texts, used by scholars worldwide.
  • Government of India Initiatives: Projects under the Sanskrit Promotion Scheme, the National Mission for Manuscripts, and various digitization drives have made parts of Sanskrit literature available online.

However, these efforts—though valuable—are fragmented, slow, and limited in scope. The scale of Sanskrit literature is massive, and manual digitization is not fast enough to cover it all.

At the same time, Sanskrit faces deeper digital challenges:

  • Data Scarcity: Beyond GRETIL and a few open sources, there is no large-scale, standardized corpus.
  • OCR Limitations: Existing OCR tools are not optimized for Sanskrit’s unique structure (shlokas, Sandhi, mixed Sanskrit–Hindi pages).
  • Morphological Complexity: Sandhi rules and compound formation make tokenization extremely challenging.
  • Lack of Modern AI Tools: Tokenizers, parsers, pretrained embeddings, and LLMs are either non-existent or in early stages.
  • Low Commercial Incentive: Unlike spoken languages, Sanskrit lacks industry funding, slowing development further.

This means that progress is being made—but not fast enough.

With the help of modern machine learning technologies, we can accelerate and automate this process:

  • Machine learning can scale OCR to thousands of texts.
  • NLP models can analyze morphology automatically, reducing the need for manual annotation.
  • TTS/STT systems can preserve oral traditions digitally.
  • LLMs can unlock semantic search, translation, and knowledge extraction at a scale not possible before.

Why We Need a Research Infrastructure

To keep Sanskrit relevant in the digital era, we must move beyond small, fragmented projects and build a robust research infrastructure.

Such an infrastructure would:

  • Preserve ancient knowledge in searchable, machine-readable form.
  • Enable computational research into Sanskrit grammar, philosophy, and science.
  • Allow cross-lingual applications (Sanskrit ↔ Hindi ↔ English).
  • Provide resources for LLMs and AI models to integrate Sanskrit knowledge into global systems.

Infrastructure is not just about digitization—it is about creating a pipeline where raw texts become structured, analyzable, and AI-ready resources.

Machine Learning Domains for Sanskrit Research

To build this pipeline, we must expand resources across several key domains:

Optical Character Recognition (OCR)

Goal: Convert printed books and manuscripts into extractable texts.

Challenges: Poor scans, mixed-language pages, continuous shlokas.

Impact: Provides the raw text that powers all downstream tasks.
